# Computational Trust Engineering

> **A proposed engineering discipline for justified operational trust.**

Civil engineering studies structures under physical load.

Electrical engineering studies energy and electromagnetism.

Computer science studies computation.

Computational Trust Engineering studies how justified confidence is created, maintained, consumed, challenged and lost in systems that observe reality and support consequential decisions.

## The problem

Modern organisations increasingly depend upon software, automation, data and AI. These systems can produce answers faster than organisations can verify them.

The limiting factor is therefore no longer access to information or inference alone.

It is justified confidence.

A system may be highly available, secure and intelligent while still producing decisions that cannot be trusted.

## Definition

**Computational Trust Engineering** is the discipline of designing, implementing and operating systems in which material claims, recommendations and decisions remain connected to evidence, provenance, uncertainty, authority and observable reality.

It treats trust as an engineered, dynamic and reviewable property rather than a declared state.

## Foundational postulates

### 1. Trust cannot be created by declaration

It must be earned through evidence, behaviour and verification.

### 2. Trust cannot be demonstrated without traceability

A conclusion that cannot be connected to its basis is an assertion, not a trustworthy result.

### 3. Trust does not transfer automatically

Trust in a source, model or person does not make every output trustworthy. The transfer must be justified for the specific context.

### 4. Trust decays

Evidence becomes stale. Conditions change. People leave. Models drift. Dependencies are replaced. Trust must therefore be maintained.

### 5. Decisions consume trust

The more consequential or irreversible a decision, the stronger and fresher its trust basis must be.

### 6. Evidence is the smallest reviewable unit of trust

Trust emerges from evidence relationships, not from confidence labels alone.

## Trust is multidimensional

Trust is not one number. Relevant dimensions include:

- evidential trust
- source trust
- model trust
- operational trust
- identity trust
- procedural trust
- temporal trust
- dependency trust
- authority trust
- ethical and legal legitimacy

An aggregate score may support prioritisation, but it must not conceal the dimensions or assumptions beneath it.

## Trust fields

Trust is relational and contextual. A source may be trustworthy for one purpose and unsuitable for another. A model may perform well in one environment and fail after conditions change.

A **trust field** describes how justified confidence varies across actors, evidence, claims, dependencies, time and mission context.

This is an engineering hypothesis rather than established physical law. Its purpose is to provide a rigorous language for analysing where trust is strong, weak, decaying or unjustifiably transferred.

## Trust potential

Trust potential is the capacity of available evidence, controls and relationships to support future justified decisions.

High trust potential may arise from:

- independently corroborated evidence
- strong provenance
- known detection capability
- tested dependencies
- clear authority
- transparent models
- recent verification

Potential is not the same as realised trust. It becomes relevant only when connected to a specific claim or decision.

## Trust momentum

Repeated verified performance can create trust momentum: a growing expectation that a system will continue to behave reliably.

Momentum is useful but dangerous. It may cause organisations to overlook changed conditions or weak current evidence.

Past success is evidence, not immunity from review.

## Trust inertia

Trust inertia is the tendency of an established belief, system or institution to remain trusted after its evidential basis has weakened.

Examples include:

- obsolete risk assumptions
- inherited supplier confidence
- models used outside their validated context
- controls assumed effective because they once passed an audit

Trust inertia is a major source of hidden organisational risk.

## Trust debt

Unjustified trust creates **trust debt**.

Trust debt accumulates when an organisation:

- accepts conclusions without provenance
- ignores contradictions
- relies on untested dependencies
- conceals uncertainty
- substitutes authority for evidence
- permits temporary exceptions to become permanent assumptions

Like technical debt, trust debt may accelerate short-term delivery while increasing future failure cost.

Unlike ordinary technical debt, it can also corrupt the organisation's understanding of reality.

## The mission equation

```text
Reality
  ↓
Observation
  ↓
Evidence
  ↓
Knowledge
  ↓
Understanding
  ↓
Decision
  ↓
Action
  ↓
Outcome
  ↓
Learning
```

Computational Trust Engineering studies the integrity of every transition.

A trustworthy outcome requires more than a correct algorithm. It requires a defensible chain from reality to action and back to learning.

## Engineering objectives

A Computational Trust Engineering practice should enable organisations to:

- identify what a conclusion depends upon
- determine whether evidence is sufficient and current
- expose uncertainty and contradiction
- prevent unjustified trust transfer
- measure broken provenance and dependency paths
- set trust thresholds proportional to consequence
- preserve human accountability
- verify whether claimed actions actually occurred
- detect and repay trust debt

## Relationship to AI

AI intensifies the need for this discipline because it can produce plausible output without a corresponding increase in truth.

AI may contribute evidence, hypotheses and analysis. It must not receive authority merely because its output is fluent or statistically confident.

The central question is not:

> Is the AI intelligent?

It is:

> Is this conclusion sufficiently justified for this mission and consequence?

## Closing vision

The future will not be limited primarily by a lack of AI.

It will be limited by a lack of justified confidence in what humans and machines jointly produce.

Mission Framework exists to make that confidence engineerable, reviewable and accountable.
